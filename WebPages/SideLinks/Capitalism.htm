THE GOSPEL OF THE MARKETPLACE

By Kenneth L. Woodward

Eleven years ago, Michael Novak set out in "The Spirit of Democratic Capitalism" to prove that capitalism "rightly understood" is compatible with Roman Catholic social doctrine. This was no small achievement, given the modern papacy's century-old suspicion of capitalism as the invention of Protestant individualism...Never mind that by the 1980's Catholics were as likely as Protestants to be heads of major corporations, or to vote for Republicans. Mr. Novak's sin, if sin it be, was to bring a converts enthusiasm to capitalism's entrepreneurial spirit, and to confer a lay theologian's blessings on it. Worse, in his critic's eyes, he did all this from the sheltered confines of the American Enterprise Institute, a not-for-profit Washington think tank financed by captains of industry.

In essence, Mr. Novak argued that democratic capitalism is morally superior to socialism: that the triangular nexus of a market economy, a democratic polity and a pluralistic culture not only produces a higher standard of living but is also more consonant with Christian values than the command economies and socialistic policies of Eastern Europe. What he wouldn't do was square his conclusions with the received wisdom of his own religious tradition. Paul VI...was no friend of American capitalism or of multinational corporations, and John Paul II, at that point was advocating (with his Polish homeland very much in mind) a modified form of socialism: workers sharing in the ownership of productive means while state planners insure a just distribution of goods and services. Rome, Mr. Novak concluded, still had much to learn from the American experience in political economy.

Socialism is now history in Eastern Europe, and John Paul II himself has embraced market economics with an ardor that signals a new departure in papal thought...

In Mr. Novak's view, Catholic ethics brings two dowries to its betrothal of capitalism: an appreciation for the "social dimensions of the free economy" and rich understanding of human inventiveness and creativity, which he celebrates as "the spirit of capitalism."

By the first, he means business and other voluntary associations that capitalism necessarily brings into existence in order to produce goods, services, and profits. In calling attention to these associations, Mr. Novak hopes to show that capitalism is inherently social, not individualistic: that by nourishing the developments of institutions and the personal virtues (of thrift, honesty and practical wisdom) necessary to sustain them,capitalism enriches the social and moral life of its constituents.

By the second, Mr. Novak means that capitalism is fundamentally as much an exercise in human creativity as are literature, science and the arts. Imagination, it should be said, has long been a central concern in Mr. Novak's writings on theology, philosophy, education, literature and sports. But now that John Paul II has declared that all workers are called to be "co-creators" with God, Mr. Novak is moved to declare that "in fact, the true moral strength of capitalism lies in its promotion of human creativity," and that a democratic capitalist society, however imperfect, is "perhaps the most responsive to the social implications of the Gospels yet developed by the human race.
