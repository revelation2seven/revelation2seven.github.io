This is a book about the historical Jesus, the Mediterranean peasant who traveled through THE VILLAGES OF GALILEE AND JUDEA and was ULTIMATELY EXECUTED BY THE ROMAN AUTHORITIES. It is an attempt to emphasize one neglected aspect of Jesus' mission, THE IMPERIAL WORLD IN WHICH HE LIVED AND WHICH HE STRUGGLED AGAINST. I intend to argue that Jesus' message was not simply a spiritual message intended for individuals, but that it was also a social and political message meant to be lived out in the structures of society.

The first part of the book BROADLY OUTLINES THE IMPERIAL CONTEXT, and the second part considers different aspects of Jesus' mission in light of that context. The final chapter considers the early church in that light as well. It should be stressed that what follows is a general overview. For a more detailed and academic version of what follows, the reader is invited to read my manuscript, "The Jesus Revolution: A Socio-Political Reading of the Gospel."

In addition to the New Testament, I have drawn heavily on the work of the first-century historian Josephus, particularly in the first half. Josephus provides important information about the politics of Jesus' day. For many of my arguments, I am indebted to some very gifted writers whose works appear in the bibliography.

Part One
The Kingdom of the World...

Chapter One
Caesar's Kingdom

The Gospel of Mark opens with the caption, "THE BEGINNING OF THE GOSPEL OF JESUS CHRIST, THE SON OF GOD" (1:1, NASB).

...Thou art my son, This Day have I begotten thee...Psalm 2:7; Hebrews 12.

SEPARATED FROM MARK'S WORLD BY TIME, DISTANCE, AND CULTURE, it's difficult to grasp how subversive this caption must have sounded in the first-century Mediterranean world. THAT ONE SENTENCE IS FILLED WITH LOADED POLITICAL TERMS, AND IT CARRIES AN OPEN CHALLENGE TO THE ONE MAN WHO HAD ALREADY BEEN PROCLAIMED THE SON OF GOD: CAESAR.

But exhort one another daily, WHILE IT IS CALLED TO DAY; lest any of you be hardened through the deceitfulness of sin.

For we are made partakers witjh Christ, IF WE HOLD THE BEGINNING OF OUR CONFIDENCE STEADFAST UNTO THE END.

While it is said, TO DAY IF YE WILL HEAR HIS VOICE, harden not your hearts as in the day of provocation...(Hebrews 3:13-15; Isaiah 66:6-9).

The word "gospel" itself is a political term. The Greek word means "good news." In the Roman empire, "good news" or "glad tidings" about a "son of god" meant an announcement about the birth, victory, or accession of an emperor. There was in fact no title too exalted to ascribe to the emperor. He was "patron," "benefactor" (cp. Luke 22:25), "savior," "son of a god," and even "god." During the time that the Gospel of John was written, the emperor Domitian required his subjects to address him as "Lord and God."

Read Rome, think America...See 2 Thessalonians 2:1-12...America is of the Seven, but it is the Eighth Western Empire to rise over God's kingdom on earth, Revelation 17:9-14 (KJV).

The first emperor of the consolidated Roman territories, Octavian, was granted the distinguished title of "Augustus" or "Reverend" by the Senate. By defeating Antony and Cleopatra at the Battle of Actium, Octavian had put an end to Rome's civil wars. As a result, he became the savior, the one who established world peace, the pax Romana or Peace of Rome. He was "the Beginning of all things," the awaited one who ushered in a new age. AT LEAST THAT WAS THE IMPERIAL PROPAGANDA, THE ROMAN GOSPEL. Many of the peoples who were subjugated by Rome felt very differently. The peasants and artisans of Galilee and Judea certainly felt differently. The author of one protest book, 4 Ezra, wrote:

... you have held the world in subjection in great terror, and the whole earth with harsh oppression, and you have lived so long in the world with deceit; and you have not judged the earth with truth. You have persecuted the meek and hurt the peacable, you have hated those who tell the truth, and loved liars, and you have destroyed the dwellings of the fruitful, and torn down the walls of those who did you no harm. And your insolence has reached the Most High and your pride the Mighty One. ... Therefore you will surely disappear ... so that the whole earth may be freed from your violence, and be refreshed and relieved, and hope for the justice and mercy of him who made her. (11:40-46).

Enter not into the path of wicked men, and go not in the way of evil men.

Avoid it, pass not by it, turn from it, and pass away.

For they sleep not, except they have done mischief; and their sleep is taken away, unless they cause some to fall.

For they eat the bread of wickedness, and drink the wine of violence.

BUT THE PATH OF THE JUST IS AS A SHINING LIGHT, THAT SHINETH MORE AND MORE UNTO THE PERFECT DAY.

The way of the wicked is in darkness, they know not at what they stumble...(Proverbs 4:14-19).

Malachi, chapter 4.

NOW is the judgment of this world: NOW shall the prince of This world be cast out...

Then Jesus said unto them, Yet a little while is the Light with you. Walk while ye have the Light, lest Darkness come upon you: for he that walketh in Darkness knoweth not whither he goeth.

While ye have the Light, believe in the Light, that ye may be children of Light...(John 12:31-36).

One doesn't have to look too far to find that type of criticism in the New Testament. In the book of Revelation, Rome is depicted as A"WOMAN DRUNK WITH THE BLOOD OF THE SAINTS" (17:6, KJV). In 1 Thessalonians 5:3, Paul criticizes the pax Romans (Apply the words to America at the time of the collapse of the Soviet Union): "When they shall say, Peace and security,' then sudden destruction will come upon them as travail comes upon a woman with child, and they shall not escape" (KJV). But the New Testament's criticism of Rome is nowhere more challenging THAN IN ITS CONSISTENT CONTRASTS BETWEEN CAESAR AND CHRIST.

Luke 2:1 states that Caesar Augustus ordered a census of "all the inhabited earth." Right from the beginning of Jesus' story, Luke has focused our attention ON THE POWERFUL WORLD LEADER, THE LORD OF THE EARTH. Then he narrates a story about Jesus being born in a humble manger (Luke 2:7). The real Lord of the earth is not to be found on a mighty throne in Rome, but in a small town on the fringe of the empire. Luke 2:14 proclaims peace on earth as a result of Jesus' birth, not as a result of Caesar. Luke 2:11 indicates that it is not Caesar but Jesus who is the long-awaited Lord and Savior.

Chapter Two
Politics and Poverty

According to Luke 2, Caesar is a sham (an image). He's not the real thing. HIS RULE IS BASED NOT ON LOVE, BUT ON FEAR AND OPPRESSION. HE BUILDS HIS EMPIRE ON THE BACKS OF OTHERS.

Now, how could the emperor effectively administer such a large empire? He couldn't. The empire lacked the infrastructure and resources to actually run an empire that surrounded the entire Mediterranean Sea. As a result, Rome often ruled through client kings (Governors). Let the subjugated nation keep its own local rulers, the Romans reasoned. As long as the rulers paid the necessary tribute to Rome and remained obedient to the emperor, they were fine. Of course, that tribute came from the taxes of the local residents. Herod the Great and his heirs, the client kings and tetrarchs beholden to Rome, were free to line their own pockets and expand their own holdings by collecting extra taxes and passing along the required amount to Rome. Their clients in turn enriched themselves the same way, by skimming off the proceeds of those underneath them in the pecking order. This economic arrangement is reflected in Jesus' parable of the unmerciful servant in Matthew 18:23-35 and parable of the shrewd servant in Luke 16:1-8. By some estimates, the total financial obligation forced onto the peasants through rent, taxes, tribute, tolls, and tithes, was somewhere near forty percent of their incomes. The burden was immense. As a result, by the time many peasants had fulfilled their obligations to the government, they lacked the money to fulfill their obligations to the temple.

Lacking the enforcement mechanism of Roman troops, temple authorites responded to this inability through censure, arguing that those who were in dire financial straights were unclean, and that the reason for their poverty and social marginalization was that they had not fulfilled their obligations to God. WITH SO MUCH MONEY FLOWING INTO THE JERUSALEM TEMPLE, it should not be surprising that the temple was more than the nation's religious center. It was a formidable political and economic stronghold as well. In fact it functioned as the national bank, providing capital to aristocratic elites who used it to further their own fortunes.

Matthew, chapter 24 (KJV).

The way people became rich in Galilee and Judea was by seizing other peoples' property. This created a downward social spiral for a large number of farmers. WHEN THEIR FINANCIAL OBLIGATIONS BECAME TOO GREAT, THEY HAD TO BORROW MONEY FROM THE ARISTOCRATIC ELITES. When they were unable to pay their debts, their lands were seized, and they were downgraded to tenants. When they were unable to pay their rent, they were further downgraded to the status of day laborers, finding temporary work on a day-to-day basis. Once they were unable to find work, they became beggars. From the time they hit the street, they could expect a short and brutal life. Jesus alluded to each of these stages in the downward spiral in his parables about tenants, day laborers, and beggars.

HAD THE JUBILEE LAWS BEEN ENFORCED IN JESUS' DAY, THIS TERRIBLE POVERTY COULD HAVE BEEN PREVENTED. According to these laws, debts had to be forgiven, land returned, and slaves released, every fiftieth year. But by using a legal loophole created by the rabbi Hillel, lenders were able to refuse making loans unless debtors agreed to forego their Jubilee rights. THE PROMISED LAND QUICKLY BECAME A LAND OF GREAT PROMISE FOR A TINY MAJORITY AND A LAND OF CRUSHING IMPOVERISHMENT FOR THE GREAT MAJORITY.

IT WAS IN THIS ENVIRONMENT THAT JESUS EMERGED PROCLAIMING THE BEGINNING OF THE ULTIMATE JUBILEE (Luke 4:16-21) and undermining the temple's authority by dispensing forgiveness of sin free of charge. He taught people to break the cycle of debt in their own relationships (cf. Luke 6:34) by praying "forgive us our sins, as we have also forgiven everyone who is indebted to us" (Luke 11:4, NASB). AND THAT WAS JUST THE BEGINNING.

Chapter Three
The Resistance

Before considering the scope of Jesus' ministry it may be helpful to consider one other aspect of Jesus' political and social context: The tradition of resistance in Judea and Galilee. Within one generation of Jesus' execution, the people of Judea and Galilee had been pushed to the breaking point. They rebelled against Rome in a tragic war that ended with a crushing defeat and the destruction of the temple in Jerusalem. There had also been protests and uprisings about a generation before Jesus' ministry. Near the end of King Herod's reign, two scribes named Judas and Matthias led a group of young men in an act of civil disobedience. Their grievance was that HEROD HAD ERECTED AN IDOLATROUS GOLDEN EAGLE OVER THE GATE OF THE TEMPLE. They tore the eagle down in an act of protest, and as a result they were arrested and burned alive. Just three decades later, JESUS WOULD ALSO PERFORM AN ACT OF CIVIL DISOBEDIENCE IN THE TEMPLE WHICH WOULD LEAD TO HIS ARREST AND EXECUTION, although his protest would be more penetrating.

Herod himself was an oppressive and ruthless tyrant. According to Matthew, Herod ordered the execution of toddlers in Bethlehem because of a rumor about the birth of the promised king of Israel (2:1-8,13-18). HE WOULD NOT TOLERATE ANY THREAT TO HIS THRONE. According to Josephus, he killed three of his own sons in order to protect it. HE BRUTALLY SQUASHED ALL DISSENT, SPIED ON CIVILIANS, FORCED THEM TO TAKE AN OATH OF LOYALTY, AND RESTRICTED THEIR RIGHT TO ASSEMBLY.

According to Matthew, Jesus' family fled to Egypt and returned only after Herod's death, which happened in 4 B.C.E. (2:19-21). However, upon hearing that Herod's oldest son Archelaus was ruling Judea, JOSEPH TOOK HIS FAMILY NORTH INTO GALILEE (vv. 22,23), which was ruled by Herod Antipas, another one of Herod's sons. Herod's sons had themselves been involved in no little dispute over their father's kingdom. Herod's desire had been that Archelaus would succeed him, but Archelaus was inexperienced and the emperor was uncertain of his abilities. Shortly after Herod had died, the people of Judea presented numerous grievances to Archelaus. They asked for a reduction in their taxes, FOR THE RELEASE OF POLITICAL PRISONERS (Mumia and Leonard Peltier), and for the punishment of those responsible for the executions of Judas and Matthias. Initially Archelaus pretended to be sensitive to their concerns, but he was in too big a hurry to go to Rome to make his case that he should be the next king. Unable to negotiate peacefully with the protestors, he panicked and dispatched his soldiers, killing thousands.

Shortly thereafter, Archelaus and Antipas had gone to Rome to argue their cases before Caesar...During that time, the procurator of Syria presumptuously seized the palace in Jerusalem, ordered an accounting of the treasury resources, and tried to take control of the fortresses. The result was even more violence. One of Herod's cousins led two thousand former soldiers in revolt. Meanwhile, MESSIANIC PRETENDERS EMERGED from both Galilee and Judea. Simon, one of Herod's slaves, proclaimed himself king and burned the palace in Jericho. A large shepherd named Athronges also claimed the throne, and along with his four brothers conducted an effective guerilla warfare campaign. Meanwhile, Judas, son of Ezekias, stormed Sepphoris in Galilee and confiscated all of the city's weapons. Each of these uprisings was brutally suppressed. ALL THE INHABITANTS OF SEPPHORIS (The Ten Sephirot) were sold into slavery and the city was burned. According to Matthew, this was about the time that Joseph, Mary, and Jesus moved to a small peasant village within walking distance of Sepphoris (Matt. 2:19-23). After destroying Sepphoris, the Romans marched south to Jerusalem AND CRUCIFIED ABOUT TWO THOUSAND REBELS (1 Corinthians 2:1-8)..

Caesar decided to make Archelaus not the king, but the ethnarch of Judea, Idumea, and Samaria, with the potential of becoming a king if he ruled well. Herod Antipas was given Galilee and Perea, and their brother Philip was given other territories. Jesus' family had good reason to avoid Archelaus. His cruel reign didn't last more than ten years. He ruled so poorly, in fact, Caesar banished him and turned Judea into a subprovince of Rome under the authority of Quirinius, governor of Syria (cf. Luke 2:2). From that time on the Judeans had to pay a heavy tribute directly to Caesar, a vivid reminder of their status as an occupied people. It was this issue that was later brought to Jesus by the authorities who wanted to trap him (Mark 12:13-17 // Matt. 22:15-22 // Luke 20:20-26).

Chapter Four
Nonviolent Resistance

Not all forms of resistance against the imperial occupation were violent. There were also nonviolent protests that are particularly noteworthy. The following examples all come from the first-century historian Josephus. One of the most intriguing tactics of nonviolent resistance happened at the beginning of Pontius Pilate's administration in Judea. The year was 26. Jesus was to begin his ministry shortly. Pilate moved his soldiers from Caesarea to Jerusalem for the winter. As was their custom, they brought with them busts of the emperor on their army standards. The Judeans, regarding this as an act of idolatry, rushed to Caesarea and implored Pilate to have the busts removed. When he refused, they prostrated themselves around his palace for five days. When Pilate called for the soldiers, the protesters bared their necks, courageously indicating their willingness to die rather than transgress God's law. Amazed at their zeal, Pilate called off his soldiers and complied with their demands, moving the images from Jerusalem to Caesarea.

Not long thereafter, Jesus taught crowds of peasants to turn the other cheek when they were struck. Turning the other cheek is not exactly the same thing as baring the neck, but as we will see later the similarity of the principle is striking. An even more remarkable demonstration of nonviolent resistance happened fifteen years later, during the reign of Caligula. Disturbed about the fact that the Judeans had built no temples to honor him, Caligula sent Petronius as legate of Syria to erect a statue of him in the Jerusalem temple. An idol representing the emperor in the Jerusalem temple would clearly have been an abomination. The people were naturally alarmed. Thousands turned out to meet Petronius at Ptolemais, and even more met him at Tiberius. They assured him that if he proceeded with the plan to erect the emperor's statue in the temple, they would cease all agricultural production. THEY WERE IN EFFECT THREATENING A MASS CAMPAIGN OF NONCOOPERATION. The result of such A WORK STOPPAGE would have proved disastrous. The show of solidarity convinced Petronius to abandon his mission. As it happened, the emperor died before the edict could be enforced anyway. BUT THE REMARKABLE SUCCESS OF THE PEOPLE'S CAMPAIGN OF NONCOOPERATION CANNOT GO UNNOTICED.

Remember the Sabbath Day to keep it holy.

SIX days shalt thou labour, and do all thy work.

But the SEVENTH DAY is the Sabbath of the Lord thy God: IN IT THOU SHALT NOT DO ANY (SERVILE) WORK, thou, nor thy son, nor thy daughter, thy manservant, nor thy maidservant, nor thy cattle, nor thy stranger that is within thy gates.

For in Six days the Lord made Heaven and Earth, the Sea, and all that is in them, AND RESTED THE SEVENTH DAY: wherefore the Lord blessed the Sabbath Day, and hallowed it...(Exodus 20:8-11; Hebrews 4:1-12).

Now this event happened after the time of JESUS AND before the public mission of PAUL (A great mystery...Galatians 1:11-16). This naturally raises the question of where the Christians were in all of this. Were there Judean and Galilean Christians who joined their compatriots in trying to prevent the erection of the abomination in the temple? We do know that the earliest Christians continued to worship in the temple and were as concerned about idolatry as any other law-abiding Judean, but ultimately we can't know for sure to what extent Christians may have participated in the anti-imperial protest.

Interestingly, another type of protest during this time involved the best known Christian in Jerusalem: James, the brother of Jesus and leader of the Jerusalem church. Just after the death of the Roman procurator in the year 62, Herod Agrippa II appointed Ananus as high priest. Ananus, the son of the priest who together with Caiaphas condemned Jesus, ILLEGALLY CONVENED THE SANHEDRIN TO TRY JAMES AND SOME OTHER CHRISTIANS. He accused them of violating Moses' law and had them stoned. The people of Jerusalem were horrified. They complained both to the new procurator and to Herod Agrippa II. The procurator chastised Ananus, but Agrippa responded to the complaints by deposing him from the priesthood. The protests of the people were successful in removing an unjust priest from the highest office.

Seven days thou shalt make an atonement for the altar, and sanctify it; and it shall be an altar most holy: whatsoever toucheth the altar shall be holy (subject to the ordinances of the Temple).

Now this is that which thou shalt offer upon the altar; TWO LAMBS OF THE FIRST YEAR DAY BY DAY CONTINUALLY.

THE ONE LAMB THOU SHALT OFFER IN THE MORNING: AND THE OTHER LAMB THOU SHALT OFFER AT EVEN...(Exodus29:37-39; Numbers 28:1-4; 1 Kings 18:36-38; Psalm 141:2).

For whom He did foreknow, He also did predestinate to be conformed to the image of His son, that he might be the Firstborn of many...

As it is written FOR THY SAKE WE ARE KILLED ALL THE DAY LONG: WE ARE ACCOUNTED AS SHEEP FOR THE SLAUGHTER...(Romans 8:33-37; Isaiah 53:7).

What is so interesting about this episode is not only that it illustrates a very basic tactic of nonviolent protest -- basically, petitioning the authorities -- but also that it involved Jesus' disciples in Jerusalem. If the disciples in Jerusalem were anywhere near as numerous as Acts suggests, I think it is highly likely that at least some of the protesters were themselves followers of Jesus.

Of course, not all nonviolent protests ended so ideally. One of the better known prophetic protestors, JOHN THE BAPTIST, WAS EXECUTED SHORTLY AFTER THE BEGINNING OF JESUS' CAREER. (2 Corinthians 12:1-5). John's message was one of imminent judgment in the land: "INDEED THE AXE IS ALREADY LAID TO THE ROOT OF THE TREES" (Luke 3:9 // Matt. 3:10, NASB). IT WAS AN APOCALYPTIC MESSAGE, AS POLITICAL AS IT WAS SPIRITUAL (Ezekiel 31; Daniel 4; Matthew 7:13-20). John criticized Herod Antipas, not just for illegally marrying his brother's wife Herodias, but also "because of all the wicked things which Herod had done" (Luke 3:19, NASB). HEROD RESPONDED PREDICTABLY BY HAVING JOHN ARRESTED (3:20).

Most things in the world, as long as their inner parts are hidden, stand upright and live. If they are revealed they die, as is illustrated by the visible man: as long as the intestines of a man are hidden, the man is alive. When his intestines are exposed and come out of him, the man will die. SO ALSO WITH A TREE: WHILE ITS ROOT IS HIDDEN IT SPROUTS AND GROWS. IF ITS ROOT IS EXPOSED THE TREE DRIES UP. So it is with every birth that is in the world, not only with the revealed but with the hidden. FOR SO LONG AS THE ROOT OF WICKEDNESS IS HIDDEN, IT IS STRONG. BUT WHEN IT IS RECOGNIZED IT IS DISSOLVED. WHEN IT IS REVEALED IT PERISHES. THAT IS WHY THE WORD SAYS, ALREADY THE AX IS LAID AT THE ROOT OF THE TREES (Mth. 3:10). It will not merely cut--what is cut sprouts again--BUT THE AX PENETRATES DEEPLY UNTIL IT BRINGS UP THE ROOT. Jesus pulled out the root of the whole place, while others did it only partially...The Gospel of Philip.

The story of John's execution in the Gospels is well known: Herod promised SALOME anything she wanted, and on behalf of her mother she demanded the head of John the Baptist on a platter. It is important to remember, however, that Herod wanted John dead anyway. According to Matthew 14:5, the only reason Herod had not already killed him was because "he feared the crowd, because they regarded John as a prophet" (NASB). According to Josephus, Herod executed John because he feared that he could cause a full-fledged rebellion...

Do you not know that the Head of Prophecy was cut off with John?

I said, Lord, can it be possible to remove the head of prophecy?

He said, When you come to know what Head means, and that prophecy issues from the Head, then understand the saying of The Head was removed...(The Apocryphon of James; Psalm 74:5-9).

When he heard of John's death, Jesus retreated first to a secluded place (Mark 6:30-32 // Matt. 14:13), then to Bethsaida, just outside of Herod Antipas' jurisdiction (Mark 6:45). Later, some Pharisees informed Jesus that Herod wanted to kill him as well (Luke 13:31). JESUS' RESPONSE, AND HIS NONVIOLENT PROTESTS WHICH LED TO HIS OWN MARTYRDOM, BECAME THE VERY FOUNDATION OF CHRISTIANITY. (1 Corinthians 3:12-17).

Chapter Five
Herod Antipas and Jesus

Two notable men in Galilee in the first half of the first century provide a study in contrasts. The first was the tetrarch Herod Antipas, son of Herod the Great. The second was Jesus of Nazareth, who as a toddler narrowly escaped death at the hands of Herod the Great. Herod Antipas and Jesus of Nazareth were natural antagonists. Jesus' messiahship was as much of a threat to Herod Antipas as it was to Herod the Great. Jesus warned his disciples about Herod, saying "Watch out! BEWARE OF THE LEAVEN OF THE PHARISEES AND THE LEAVEN OF HEROD" (Mark 8:15, NASB). It may not be immediately apparent what the Pharisees and Herod had in common. Indeed, the Pharisees belonged to a much lower social class, were far more devout in their faith, and were far less accomodating to Rome. But they were dependent upon the upper classes for what little authority they had. It was because of their status as retainers to the aristocratic classes that the Pharisees joined Herod's supporters, the Herodians, in antagonizing Jesus (Mark 3:6; 12:13 // Matt. 12:16).

According to Luke 13:31-33:...some Pharisees approached , saying to Him, 'Go away, leave here, for Herod wants to kill you.' And He said to them, 'Go and tell that fox, 'Behold, I cast out demons and perform cures today and tomorrow, AND THE THIRD DAY I SHALL BE PERFECTED. Nevertheless, I must journey on TODAY and TOMORROW and THE NEXT DAY; FOR IT CANNOT BE THAT A PROPHET PERISH OUTSIDE OF JERUSALEM"...

. This passage conveys a great deal of information about Herod and Jesus. First, it indicates that Herod wanted to kill him, which reinforces the point that JESUS WAS A REAL POLITICAL THREAT TO THE EXISTING SOCIAL ORDER. Second, it indicates Jesus' attitude toward both Herod and the Roman authority, as well as his evaluation of his own mission. Jesus fully expected to be executed as a social prophet, and as prophets tended to be killed in Jerusalem HE HAD TO GO THERE TO CONFRONT THE AUTHORITIES. But there is another point that deserves to be explored here. The first involves his immediate response to the Pharisees. In what way did Jesus' healing people and casting out demons constitute a defiance of Herod? His Beelzebub controversy with the Pharisees two chapters earlier in Luke's Gospel provides a clue. There, Jesus had said that "if I cast out demons by the finger of God, then the kingdom of God has come upon you" (Luke 11:20, NASB). Exorcism -- the defeat of the powers which kept the people oppressed -- was understood as a sign of the coming of the kingdom of God.

But there is something else about Luke 11:20 that helps to illustrate the political nature of that kingdom. The language of "the finger of God" comes from the Exodus narrative (Ex. 8:19). The story of the liberation of the Hebrews from slavery and oppression in Egypt was an eminently political story with very concrete, social consequences. The finger of God bringing about a new Exodus and a new kingdom was no less threatening to the power structures of Jesus' own day.

Jesus' first recorded words of preaching in both Matthew's and Mark's Gospels involve this political challenge: "THE TIME IS FULFILLED, AND THE KINGDOM OF GOD IS AT HAND; repent and believe in the gospel" (Mark 1:15). In Luke's Gospel, Jesus goes so far as to say, "I must preach the kingdom of God to the other cities also, for I was sent for this purpose" (Luke 4:43, NASB). What made this challenge most serious is that Jesus implied he was not only the herald of this new kingdom, but its king. When he rode into Jerusalem on a donkey during Passover, he knew exactly what he was doing. He was invoking the memory of Zechariah 9:9 to indicate that Israel's true king had arrived at long last.

Passover celebrated the liberation of Israel from a foreign oppressor in the distant past. THE ARRIVAL OF A NEW KING DURING THIS FESTIVAL OF LIBERATION implied a challenge to the Roman occupation, even though -- perhaps especially because -- this challenge was being made by a very different kind of king. Just what sort of king was Jesus?

Chapter Six
Forgiving the Debt

Jesus may have presented himself as a king to the peasants of Galilee and Judea, BUT HE WAS VERY CLEARLY DIFFERENT FROM THE KINGS THAT PRECEDED HIM.

There had been initial excitement over the establishment of the Hasmonean dynasty following the Maccabean revolution of the second century B.C.E., but the Hasmoneans had turned out not to be such great rulers after all. The Herodian dynasty lacked even the validity of Hasmonean heritage, and the Herodians were only puppet-rulers who answered directly to Rome. Whether Israel's rulers were natives or foreigners, the oppression remained the same.

ECONOMIC OPPRESSION in Israel was not new. The prophets had condemned ECONOMIC INJUSTICE frequently. Isaiah wrote:

The Lord enters into judgment with the elders and princes of His people,
"It is you who have devoured the vineyard;
The plunder of the poor is in your houses.
What do you mean by crushing My people,
And grinding the face of the poor?"
Declares the Lord God of hosts (3:14,15, NASB).

Similarly, Amos had written:

Therefore, because you impose heavy rent on the poor
And exact a tribute of grain from them,
Though you have built houses of well-hewn stone,
Yet you will not live in them;
You have planted pleasant vineyards, yet you will not drink their wine (5:11, NASB).

This prophetic judgment is reflected in the Magnificat, in Luke 1:53. Mary sings:

He has brought down rulers from their thrones,
And has exalted those who were humble.
He has filled the hungry with good things;
And sent away the rich empty-handed (NASB).

Jesus himself said, "Blessed are you who are poor, for yours is the kingdom of God....But woe to you who are rich, for you are receiving your comfort in full" (Luke 6:20,24, NASB). It is difficult to deny that Jesus promised liberation for the oppressed. This is reflected clearly in Luke's account of Jesus' inaugural address in Nazareth. There, Jesus reads from Isaiah the prophet:

The spirit of the Lord is upon Me,
Because He hath anointed Me to preach the gospel to the poor.
He has sent Me to heal the brokenhearted, to proclaim release to the captives,
And recovery of sight to the blind,
To set free those who are downtrodden,
To preach the Aceeptable Year of the Lord (Luke 4:18,19, quoting Isaiah 61:1,2a).

THAT LAST PHRASE IS A REFERENCE TO THE JUBILEE PROVISION OF LEVITICUS 25:10. Jubilee was an important part of Moses' law intended to prevent perpetual poverty. Chapter Seven
Counter-Kingdom

There was a distinctive social dimension to the counter-kingdom that Jesus began to build. His message was not only a message for individuals, challenging people to confess their personal sins and get their hearts right. It was also a message for whole communities (cf. Luke 10:13-15 // Matt. 11:20-23), and a challenge to become part of a renewed community.

Jesus' movement was a renewal movement. He described it as a mission "to the lost sheep of the house of Israel" (Matt. 10:6; 15:24). He chose twelve disciples, one for each of the twelve tribes of Israel (cf. Luke 22:30 // Matt. 19:28). He encouraged the development of "fictive kinship networks," that is, groups of people who relate to one another as if they are in fact brothers and sisters. He encouraged, as it were, ALTERNATIVE INSTITUTIONS TO THE IMPERIAL ORDER, telling his followers to resolve disputes among themselves instead of going to the authorities (cf. Luke 12:57-59 // Matt. 5:25,26; Luke 17:2,3 // Matt. 18:15-17; cp. 1 Cor. 6:1-8).

What was the basis of authority and leadership in this renewal movement? Jesus described the pattern to his disciples in Mark 10:42,43: You know that those who are regarded as rulers of the nations lord it over them, and their high officials exercise authority over them. IT SHALL NOT BE SO AMONG YOU. Instead, anyone who wants to become great among you must be your servant. THIS SAYING CARRIES AN ANTI-IMPERIAL POLEMIC. "Those who are regarded as rulers of the nations" or "the Gentiles" are the Romans. Luke even adds, "those who exercise authority over them call themselves Benefactors" (Luke 22:25, NIV). "Benefactor," as we have seen, was one of the titles of the Roman emperor. However, THE TRUE LEADER of the disciples WILL BE THEIR SERVANT. Jesus goes on to imply that he is their true leader specifically because he is their true servant, willing not to be served but to serve, even to die for their sakes (Mark 10:45). CAESAR TIBERIUS (The American President) WOULD BE WILLING TO SACRIFICE OTHERS TO KEEP HIS THRONE, BUT HE WOULD NOT HAVE BEEN WILLING TO DIE FOR HIS SUBJECTS (John 10:1-18). Messiah Jesus was the ultimate servant.

How did Jesus' revolutionary renewal movement look to the authorities who collaborated with the occupation government? Jesus himself told parables to illustrate what his movement was like in relation to the existing power structures. He compared the kingdom of his proclamation to the spread of a mustard plant (Mark 4:30-32 // Matt. 13:31,32 // Luke 13:18,19). He said: How shall we picture the kingdom of God, or by what parable shall we present it? It is like a mustard seed, which, when sown upon the soil, though it is smaller than all the seeds that are upon the soil, yet when it is sown, grows up and becomes larger than all the garden plants and forms large branches; so that the birds of the air can nest under its shade (Mark 4:30-32 // Matt. 13:31,32 // Luke 13:18,19, NASB).

The statement about "the birds of the air" ties the parable back to Ezekiel 17:23, 31:6, and Daniel 4:12, emphasizing the concrete political nature of the kingdom. Matthew and Luke describe the mustard plant as a "tree," probably to reinforce the allusion to Ezekiel and Daniel, but Mark's version is more accurate horticulturally since the mustard plant is not so much a tree as a shrub, and a nasty shrub at that. In fact it is a dreaded weed, partly because it is so hard to control. It rapidly takes over the whole garden and violates the principle of separating different types of seed as prescribed in Leviticus 19:19. TO THOSE IN POWER, THE COUNTER-CULTURAL KINGDOM OF GOD LOOKED LIKE THE SPREAD OF A DANGEROUS WEED!

Another parable Jesus told communicates much the same point: And again He said, "To what shall I compare the kingdom of God? It is like leaven, which a woman took and hid IN THREE MEASURES OF MEAL, until it was all leavened" (Luke 13:20,21 // Matt. 13:33; Genesis 18:1-6). There are three elements of this parable that are particularly noteworthy. The first is the fact that the woman "hid" the leaven in the dough. That implies secrecy. IF GOD'S KINGDOM IS LIKE SOMETHING (THAT WAS) HIDDEN IN A BATCH OF DOUGH, THEN IT IS A SUBVERSIVE PRESENCE. The second is the fact that leaven was an "unclean" ingredient, a symbol of sin or corruption. JESUS VIOLATED MANY PURITY LAWS AND ASSOCIATED WITH ALL THE WRONG PEOPLE, the unclean rabble. The third is the fact that three MEASURES of dough is a very large amount. That equals 50 pounds of flour, enough to make cakes for 100 people. IT IS HIGHLY LIKELY THAT SUCH A LARGE BATCH OF DOUGH WAS MEANT TO REPRESENT THE (ENTIRE) NATION.

The point of the parable, then, is that the kingdom of Jesus' proclamation spreads secretly, like wildfire, tearing down all social barriers and contaminating the whole nation. Little wonder that Jesus was brought to Pilate with the charge, "HE STIRS UP THE PEOPLE, teaching all over Judea, starting from Galilee, EVEN AS FAR AS THIS PLACE" (Luke 23:5, NASB).

The specific charges brought against Jesus are described in Luke 23:2: And they began to accuse Him, saying, "We found this man misleading our nation and forbidding to pay taxes to Caesar, and saying that He Himself is Christ, a King." From the standpoint of the authorities, Jesus certainly was misleading the nation. As we have seen, his teachings undermined the authority of not only the imperial occupation and its client rulers, but the temple authorities as well. HE WAS CLEARLY A THREATTO THE STATUS QUO. We have also considered the point that Jesus presented himself to the people of Jerusalem as Christ, the promised king, when he rode into Jerusalem during Passover on a donkey. There is substantial truth behind these charges.

What, then, of the charge that Jesus forbade the paying of taxes to Caesar? Jesus' statement, "RENDER TO CAESAR THE THINGS THAT ARE CAESAR'S, AND TO GOD THE THINGS THAT ARE GOD'S", is widely interpreted to mean that Jesus actually supported the paying of tribute to Caesar. But that type of statement would hardly have amazed Jesus' opponents, as it is portrayed as doing (Mark 12:17). The context of that statement is a debate in which the Pharisees and Herodians were trying to trap Jesus (Mark 12:13). Their question, "Is it lawful to pay a poll-tax to Caesar, or not?" was particularly clever because either a "yes" or a "no" answer would land Jesus in trouble. If he had said "no," he could have been arrested on the spot. If he had said "yes," he would have looked like a collaborator. Jesus was being asked either to openly declare his opposition to Caesar or to lie about it. He did neither.

What was Jesus' response? First, he asked his critics to show him a denarius (Mark 12:15). This automatically shifted the focus from Jesus to his critics. He did not have a denarius on him, whereas his critics did. They, not him, were therefore identified with the denarius. Second, he asked whose image and inscription was on the denarius (Mark 12:16 pars.). That question carried with it an implicit criticism. Graven images were forbidden by the Ten Commandments, and yet here was a coin with a graven image of the emperor. The inscription of the denarius of this period read, "Tiberius Caesar, Augustus, son of the divine Augustus." This coin, in other words, was an idol.

Jesus' final statement, "Render to Caesar the things that are Caesar's and to God the things that are God's," needs to be considered carefully. What things are God's? According to the Scriptures, God owns the cattle on a thousand hills (Psa. 50:10) -- in a word, everything. What then does that really leave for Caesar? Nothing except for all those little minted idols, which may be returned to him immediately and taken out of the land. Jesus' response was therefore a covert rejection of Roman authority which nevertheless was ambiguous enough to stave off the threat of imminent arrest. After all, Jesus' reply was technically acceptable, even though his real meaning lay not far from the surface. This implied meaning was not entirely lost on his accusers as it emerged as evidence against him when he was presented to the local Roman authority.

But if Jesus believed that Rome had no right to rule over Judea and Galilee, how did he propose to liberate the nation? After all, he had already rejected the power politics of the world. He had no army (Well kind of...Ephesians 1:17-23; 2 Thessalonians 1:6-10; Isaiah 66:1-8; Isaiah 63:1-4; Psalm 18:6-15; 2 Samuel 22:1-51; Deuteronomy 32: 16-42), nor any intention simply to take the place of Herod Antipas. Just what type of revolution was Jesus about?

Chapter Eight
A Different Kind of Revolution

It is clear what type of revolution Jesus was not interested in creating. In Gethsemane, after Peter had attacked the high priest's servant with a sword, Jesus said, "Put your sword back into its place; for all those who take up the sword shall perish by the sword" (Matt. 26:52). It is clear that Jesus rejected the path of violence. Instead, Jesus impressed upon his followers the importance of loving all people, including the enemy (Matt. 5:43). This teaching is truly revolutionary. God's kingdom would not arrive through the shedding of blood; Israel had "been there, done that." Previous uprisings had simply replaced one type of dictatorship with another. God's people would not find liberation through the sword. That path led to even more blood. Unconditional love was the path to true liberation.

But what, exactly, did this mean? Consider a more concrete teaching. A few verses earlier, Jesus repealed the law of retaliation, saying, "You have heard that it was said, 'Eye for eye, and tooth for tooth.' But I tell you, Do not resist an evil person" (Matt. 5:38,39). Unfortunately, this translation obscures Jesus' point. Did Jesus really say "do not resist evil?" That would be inconsistent with his own practice. Jesus resisted evil at every point. He did not hesitate to confront hypocrisy or greed, and he never hesitated to condemn injustice. What, then, did he mean? The key to unlocking this apparent contradiction lies in our translation of the Greek word for "resist," antistenai. This was a technical term from the Greek Old Testament that denoted warfare or violent resistance. Keeping this in mind, the Scholar's Version more accurately reads, "Don't react violently against the one who is evil." Jesus' followers were indeed to resist evil, but not in a violent way. They were to resist in a nonviolent way instead.

This principle is described in three illustrations which follow. First, Jesus says, "whoever slaps you on your right cheek, turn to him the other also" (v. 5:39). This statement has long been misunderstood as an example of passive nonresistance. But this could hardly have been Jesus' meaning. When he was unjustly struck by the high priest's officer, Jesus did not passively cower. Instead, he talked back and challenged him (John 18:22,23). What, then, did he mean by "turn the other cheek"? A closer consideration of the text will reveal that it does not describe passive nonresistance, but an active form of nonviolent resistance instead. Jesus is clearly not describing the blow of a closed fist, but a backhanded slap. This is apparent from the fact that Jesus specifies the right cheek. It would be very difficult for a right-handed person to punch someone in the right cheek. To strike someone on the right cheek, a right-handed person would need to swing his or her right hand in an arc from left to right, thereby striking the victim on the right cheek.

In that culture, this was not the type of blow delivered by an equal. Instead, it was the type of blow delivered by a superior to an inferior, by a master to a slave, by a soldier to a peasant. It was the type of blow intended to put someone back into his or her place. By turning the other cheek, however, the victim would make it very difficult for the offender to strike in quite that same way again. It implied standing up to the oppressor, sending the nonverbal message that no matter what the oppressor does, he or she will not break the spirit of the aggrieved party. There will be no cowering before injustice, only the power of nonviolent resistance.

The next example implies a juridicial setting: "And if someone wants to sue you, and take your shirt, let him have your coat also" (5:40). Luke reverses the order, writing "whoever takes away your coat, do not withhold your shirt from him either" (Luke 6:29). Like the statement about turning the other cheek, this statement has also been widely misunderstood. To understand the full impact of this example, consider the fact that people of that day only wore two pieces of clothing: A long tunic or shirt that covered the body, and an outer garment or cloak. For the poorest of the poor, that outer cloak doubled as a sleeping bag. For that reason, Moses' law forbade creditors from permanently taking away their cloaks (Deut. 24:10-13).

Now consider the court scene described by Jesus. The creditor sues the peasant and takes away his cloak. The peasant strips off his tunic as well and hands it over. At this point, the debtor is stark naked -- as if to say, "You're going to take my coat? Okay, have my shirt as well. Now I don't have any clothes at all. Are you happy?" It is yet another form of protest, an object lesson in oppression.

In Jesus' own case, nonviolent resistance and noncooperation meant his own death. And not just any death -- death on a cross, a grueling form of punishment reserved for revolutionaries and enemies of the state. BUT IT WAS PRECISELY THAT DEATH THAT EXPOSED EVIL AND SIN FOR WHAT IT WAS AND EMPOWERED THE SOCIAL REVOLUTION KNOWN AS THE EARLY CHURCH. Through his death on the cross and victory over death, Jesus overcame evil and oppression. His spiritually empowered disciples followed the lead of their king and continued to liberate their people in the fullest sense.

Chapter Nine
Jesus' Disciples

How faithful were Jesus' first followers, the early Christians of the first century? Did they carry on Jesus' tradition of social revolution and resisting evil with love, or did they instead spiritualize his message in favor of the imperial status quo? Considering the shape of the book of Acts may help to answer some of these questions. The first thing that can be said about the churches in Acts is that they were truly alternative communities whose very existence challenged the ways of the imperial world. By proclaiming the lordship of Jesus, they denied the lordship of Caesar. When Peter boldly addressed the "Rulers and elders of the people" (Acts 4:8) and proclaimed that "there is salvation in no one else; for there is no other name under heaven that has been given among men, by which we must be saved" (v. 12), he was undermining Rome's imperial propaganda; for whatever else that assertion may have meant, it denied the imperial claim that Caesar was savior of the world.

This assertion was followed by civil disobedience, as the disciples refused to remain silent about their rival gospel (4:18-20; 5:28,29). In a prayer meeting afterward, according to Luke, the disciples considered the implications of the the fact that Jesus was opposed both by Herod Antipas, the tetrarch who answered directly to Rome, and by Pontius Pilate, the Roman prefect (4:27). They also prayed about "signs and wonders" (v. 30), a clear allusion to the liberation of Israel from Egypt (cf. Ex. 7:3).

The very outline of Acts itself contains a revolutionary message. The book may be divided into two parts. The first part, extending from the beginning through chapter 12, chronicles the history of the church in Israel and focuses on Peter. It culminates in Peter's imprisonment under Herod Agrippa I, the last Herodian king who ruled all of Israel. The second part, extending from chapter 13 through the end of the book, chronicles the spread of the church in the empire at large and focuses on Paul. It culminates in Paul's imprisonment under Caesar Nero, THE MOST POWERFUL PERSON IN THE WORLD.

Here is the truly revolutionary part of that observation. In chapter 12, Peter is liberated from jail while the king of Israel, Herod Agrippa I, is struck down in divine judgment. Though chapter 28 does not narrate the end of Paul or of Nero, we do know that Nero came to an untimely end as well. What do Herod Agrippa I and Nero have in common? Both were powerful rulers: One the ruler of Israel, the other the ruler of the whole empire. In addition, both arrogated to themselves divine honors. (Imperialists have always been tempted to claim divine sanction for their regimes.) As a result, they were both judged. A MOVEMENT WHOSE GOD COULD DEPOSE KINGS AND EMPERORS WAS TRULY A DANGEROUS AND REVOLUTIONARY MOVEMENT.

Conclusions

Jesus ministered among impoverished peasants who struggled to survive in a world dominated by an imperial foreign oppressor. Not only were Jesus' people oppressed by a foreign government, they were oppressed by their own leaders as well. Even the temple system contributed to their poverty.

JESUS PROCLAIMED THE ADVENT OF A VERY DIFFERENT TYPE OF KINGDOM, A KINGDOM BASED ON LOVE AND NONVIOLENCE. He undermined the legitimacy of both the royal authorities and the temple authorities. HIS MOVEMENT WAS A SUBVERSIVE, SOCIAL REVOLUTION, A MOVEMENT THAT THREATENED THE IMPERIAL STATUS QUO. AS A RESULT HE WAS EXECUTED AS AN ENEMY OF THE STATE. Paradoxically, however, the occasion of his crucifixion became the very instrument that exposed and overcame evil and injustice.

AS MODERN-DAY FOLLOWERS OF JESUS, CAN WE PERCEIVE THE THREAT OF IMPERIAL OPPRESSION IN THE WORLD TODAY? DO WE HAVE THE COURAGE TO CONFRONT STRUCTURAL INJUSTICE, TO TAKE RULING AUTHORITIES TO TASK FOR OPPRESSING THE POOR? Do we feel any obligation to minister to those who are oppressed? I submit that the church has a responsibility to work for justice, a responsibility enjoined upon her by Jesus himself.
